This article will go over processes count and whether or not you need to concern yourself with lowering them.

Let's start off with the key source of information people turn to when arguing against the process counter, Zusier's Blog .

I highly recommend you check out the blog for yourself, but to summarize the key point... he ran a test where he made a single process as heavy as possible; it ended up comparable to 1,000+ Discord instances and completely locked his system — all from one process.

Then Zusier did the opposite: he created 3,000 processes that only slept. And he could still play games perfectly fine.

The conclusion he wants people to understand is that the number itself is not the problem; rather, specific processes causing load are the problem. To truly know what’s slowing down your machine, you should look at metrics like Cycles Delta and Context Switch Delta (available in Process Explorer ).

What are these measurements indicative of?

Cycles Delta:

  • The number of CPU clock cycles used between samples. Shows how much “work” the CPU is doing.

Context Switching Delta:

  • The change in context switches between samples. High deltas indicate task-switching overhead.

In simple terms: these values help you see what’s eating at your CPU, which often correlates to reduced performance.

In Process Explorer , you can enable these by right-clicking any column, selecting “Select Columns,” and enabling CPU Cycles Delta and Context Switch Delta.

What should you disable?

A great place to start is disabling startup applications. After that, close things like Spotify, Discord, browsers, or anything else running in the background. You can check some of what’s running in your tray icons.

What about disabling Windows Services?

Disabling Windows services for FPS gain is not useful — the improvement is not measurable.

The main effect of disabling services is reducing RAM usage — but the risk is enormous. You can easily break your system to the point that it won't boot without reinstalling Windows.

Event Viewer Flood

This video from Savitarax shows that disabling services can flood Event Viewer with errors. No benchmarks were provided, but he believes the constant error loop can negatively affect performance. Even if it doesn’t, it’s important to know these errors occur.

Conclusion

Don’t chase a low process number for ego. Target specific misbehaving apps using Process Explorer . Disable startup apps, close unnecessary background apps, and avoid bulk-disabling Windows services. The risks do not outweigh the gains.
